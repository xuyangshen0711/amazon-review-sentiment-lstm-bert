3. DATASET
----------------------------------------------------------------

3.1 Source and Label Construction

The dataset used in this study is drawn from the Amazon Software
Reviews corpus, part of the Amazon Product Reviews dataset released by
Ni et al. (2019). The corpus is hosted at jmcauley.ucsd.edu/data/
amazon_v2/ and covers reviews submitted to Amazon from May 1996 to
October 2018. We use the 5-core version of the Software category
(Software_5.json.gz), which retains only users and items with at least
five reviews to ensure data quality.

Each review record contains a star rating (1 to 5) and a text body.
We construct binary sentiment labels by assigning positive (label = 1)
to reviews with 4 or 5 stars, and negative (label = 0) to reviews
with 1 or 2 stars. Reviews with a 3-star rating are excluded, as their
sentiment polarity is ambiguous. After filtering, the dataset contains
11,206 reviews.

The class distribution is heavily skewed toward positive ratings,
with approximately 80.2% of reviews labeled positive. This reflects
the J-shaped distribution of product reviews documented by Hu et al.
(2009) and represents the primary data-level challenge examined in
this work.

[Table 1: Dataset Statistics]

+-------------------------------+--------+-----------+
| Category                      | Count  | % of Total|
+-------------------------------+--------+-----------+
| Total reviews (after filter)  | 11,206 | 100.0%    |
| Positive (4–5 stars, label=1) |  8,987 |  80.2%    |
| Negative (1–2 stars, label=0) |  2,219 |  19.8%    |
+-------------------------------+--------+-----------+


3.2 Data Split

The dataset is partitioned into training, validation, and test sets
at an 80/10/10 ratio. Stratified splitting is applied at both stages
to preserve the class distribution across all splits.

Both the LSTM and BERT pipelines apply the identical splitting
procedure, using the same random seed (42) and the same stratification
strategy. All models are therefore trained and evaluated on exactly
the same data partitions, making cross-model comparisons valid.

[Table 2: Data Split]

+--------------+--------+------------------+------------------+
| Split        | Total  | Positive (label=1)| Negative (label=0)|
+--------------+--------+------------------+------------------+
| Train (80%)  |  8,964 |   7,189 (80.2%)  |   1,775 (19.8%)  |
| Val   (10%)  |  1,121 |     899 (80.2%)  |     222 (19.8%)  |
| Test  (10%)  |  1,121 |     899 (80.2%)  |     222 (19.8%)  |
| Total        | 11,206 |   8,987 (80.2%)  |   2,219 (19.8%)  |
+--------------+--------+------------------+------------------+

4. METHODS
================================================================

This study compares two families of deep learning models for binary
sentiment classification: an LSTM-based model and a BERT-based model.
For LSTM, we develop three successive versions that progressively
address the class imbalance problem. For BERT, we fine-tune a
pretrained model following standard practices for sentiment
classification tasks.

[Figure recommended: side-by-side pipeline diagram for LSTM (V3)
and BERT, from raw text input to binary prediction output]


4.1 LSTM-based Models

4.1.1 Shared Preprocessing and Embedding

All three LSTM versions share the same text preprocessing pipeline.
Review text is lowercased, punctuation is removed, and stopwords are
filtered using NLTK's English stopword list. A vocabulary is built
from the training set only (words appearing fewer than two times are
excluded), yielding approximately 16,199 unique tokens. Tokens not
found in the vocabulary are mapped to a special <unk> index.

Token sequences are padded or truncated to a fixed maximum length of
256 tokens. Each token is represented using GloVe 300-dimensional
pretrained word embeddings (Pennington et al., 2014), which are kept
frozen throughout training to prevent overfitting on the relatively
small dataset. Out-of-vocabulary words are initialized with random
normal vectors (sigma = 0.6), while the padding token remains a zero
vector.


4.1.2 Version 1: Unweighted LSTM (Baseline)

The baseline model consists of a 2-layer unidirectional LSTM with a
hidden dimension of 256. A dropout layer (p = 0.5) is applied to the
output of the LSTM, followed by a fully connected layer that maps to
a single scalar output. A sigmoid activation produces the final
probability. The final hidden state of the last LSTM layer is used
as the fixed-length sentence representation.

The model is trained using binary cross-entropy loss (BCELoss) with
no class weighting. The Adam optimizer is used with a learning rate
of 1e-3. Training runs for up to 10 epochs. Early stopping is
triggered after 3 consecutive epochs without improvement in
validation loss. The classification threshold is fixed at 0.5.
The batch size is 64.


4.1.3 Version 2: Simple Weighted Loss LSTM

Version 2 introduces a weighted loss function as the only
modification to the baseline. BCELoss is replaced with
BCEWithLogitsLoss, and a pos_weight value of
num_neg / num_pos = 1775 / 7189 ≈ 0.247 is applied. The intent is
to reduce the relative influence of the majority positive class
during training. All other settings remain identical to Version 1.

As discussed in Section 5, this intervention causes the model to
collapse in the opposite direction, predicting nearly all samples as
negative. The result demonstrates that a single loss-weighting change
is insufficient when the class ratio is severe, motivating the more
comprehensive approach in Version 3.


4.1.4 Version 3: Bidirectional LSTM with Combined Mitigation

Version 3 introduces four coordinated changes designed to address
class imbalance at multiple levels simultaneously.

Architecture. The model is changed from a unidirectional LSTM to a
bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) with a hidden dimension of 128 per
direction. Rather than using only the final hidden state, max pooling
is applied over all time steps, producing a 256-dimensional
representation (128 from each direction). This allows the model to
capture the most salient features anywhere in the sequence rather than
being limited to the final position.

Balanced Sampling. PyTorch's WeightedRandomSampler is used to
construct training batches. Each training sample is assigned a weight
inversely proportional to its class frequency, ensuring that batches
contain approximately equal numbers of positive and negative samples.
This addresses the imbalance at the data level, complementing the
loss-level correction described below.

Per-sample Weighted Loss. The loss function uses BCEWithLogitsLoss
with reduction="none", which returns an individual loss value for
each sample. A class-specific weight is then applied manually before
averaging: negative samples receive weight N / (2 * N_neg) = 2.5251
and positive samples receive N / (2 * N_pos) = 0.6235, where N is
the total training set size. This inverse-frequency weighting
distributes the total loss budget evenly between the two classes.

Dynamic Threshold Tuning. Rather than using a fixed threshold of 0.5,
the optimal classification threshold is determined on the validation
set at the end of each epoch. A grid of 33 thresholds from 0.10 to
0.90 is evaluated, and the threshold maximizing macro F1 is selected.
Ties are broken in favor of the threshold with higher Class-0 recall,
to avoid a recurrence of the majority-class collapse seen in earlier
versions. The threshold selected during training is applied at test
time. The early stopping criterion is also changed from validation
loss to validation macro F1, with a patience of 4 epochs.

The Adam optimizer is used with a learning rate of 8e-4. Batch size
and maximum sequence length are unchanged at 64 and 256, respectively.


4.2 BERT-based Model

4.2.1 Tokenization

Input text is tokenized using the WordPiece tokenizer from
bert-base-uncased. The tokenizer adds the special tokens [CLS] and
[SEP] automatically. Sequences are padded or truncated to a maximum
length of 256 tokens. The batch size is set to 16, smaller than the
LSTM models due to BERT's higher memory requirements.

4.2.2 Model Architecture

The model uses the bert-base-uncased pretrained checkpoint (Devlin
et al., 2019), which contains 12 transformer encoder layers, a hidden
dimension of 768, and approximately 109 million parameters. A linear
classification head is added on top of the [CLS] token representation,
mapping from 768 dimensions to 2 output logits (negative and
positive). The final prediction is the argmax of these logits. No
class weighting is applied to the loss function.

4.2.3 Fine-tuning

The model is fine-tuned end-to-end for a maximum of 4 epochs, which
is standard for BERT fine-tuning on downstream classification tasks.
The AdamW optimizer is used with a learning rate of 2e-5 and a weight
decay of 0.01. A linear warmup scheduler increases the learning rate
linearly for the first 10% of total training steps, after which it
decays linearly back to zero. Gradient clipping with a maximum norm
of 1.0 is applied at each step. The model checkpoint with the lowest
validation loss is saved and used for final evaluation.

5. RESULTS
================================================================

All four model configurations are evaluated on the same held-out
test set of 1,121 reviews (899 positive, 222 negative). For BiLSTM
V3, the threshold of 0.100 selected on the validation set is applied
at test time. For all other models, the threshold is fixed at 0.5.

We report accuracy, per-class precision, recall, and F1 for Class-0
(negative) and Class-1 (positive), as well as macro F1. Macro F1
weights both classes equally and is the primary metric for evaluating
performance under class imbalance. Overall accuracy is reported for
reference but is not the primary criterion, as it is dominated by
the majority class.

5.1 Baseline LSTM (V1)

LSTM V1 achieves 80.37% accuracy, which appears reasonable at first
glance. However, this figure is entirely driven by the model's
behavior on the majority class. The model predicts positive for
99.67% of the samples it sees, correctly classifying 896 out of 899
positive reviews while correctly identifying only 5 out of 222
negative reviews. Class-0 recall is 2.25%, and macro F1 is 46.71%.
This is a near-degenerate classifier: its accuracy reflects the
base rate of the dataset rather than any learned discriminative
ability.


5.2 Simple Weighted LSTM (V2)

Version 2 corrects this collapse but in the wrong direction.
By applying a pos_weight of 0.247, the model heavily down-weights
positive-class loss, causing it to predict negative for almost every
sample. Class-0 recall rises to 98.65%, but Class-1 recall drops to
2.00%. Accuracy falls to 21.14%, and macro F1 drops further to
18.53%. This result illustrates that a single loss-weighting
parameter, when applied too aggressively, replaces one type of
collapse with another rather than producing balanced performance.


5.3 BiLSTM with Combined Mitigation (V3)

The combined approach in Version 3 produces substantially more
balanced results. Class-0 recall improves from 2.25% to 68.92%, an
improvement of 66.67 percentage points. Class-0 precision improves
from 62.50% to 78.46%. Macro F1 rises from 46.71% to 83.65%. At the
same time, Class-1 recall remains high at 95.33%, a modest decline
from 99.67% in V1. Overall accuracy reaches 90.10%.

These results confirm H3: the combination of architectural,
sampling, and threshold-level interventions substantially improves
minority-class performance. They also confirm H2: the unweighted
LSTM collapsed almost entirely to majority-class prediction, while
BERT (below) showed no such collapse without any imbalance-specific
treatment.


5.4 BERT

BERT achieves the strongest overall performance across all metrics.
Accuracy is 93.84%, and macro F1 is 89.83%. Class-0 recall is
78.38%, compared to 68.92% for BiLSTM V3. Class-0 F1 is 83.47%,
compared to 73.38% for V3. On the positive class, BERT also leads,
with a Class-1 F1 of 96.19% against V3's 93.92%.

These results confirm H1: BERT outperforms LSTM across all major
metrics. The performance advantage on the negative class is
particularly notable. BERT achieves higher Class-0 recall than the
improved BiLSTM V3, without any class-imbalance-specific training
intervention. This suggests that BERT's pretrained contextual
representations provide an implicit advantage in handling imbalanced
data, consistent with the interpretation offered in Section 6.
